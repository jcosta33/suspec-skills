# Evidence Appraisal

Use this reference for empirical, scientific, safety, reliability, performance, prevalence, or
causal claims. Judge whether evidence supports the exact claim; venue labels do not decide fit.

## Claim Fit

| Claim | Strong direct evidence | Common mismatch |
| --- | --- | --- |
| Standard or contract | Current specification | Tutorial paraphrase |
| API support | Versioned docs, source, executable contract | Another version's review |
| Current product behavior | Safe direct exercise under recorded conditions | Marketing copy |
| Comparative performance | Representative benchmark with method, data, variance, matched conditions | Undisclosed vendor workload |
| Causal effect | Design controlling alternatives with uncertainty | Before/after correlation |
| Prevalence | Disclosed sampling frame and denominator | Collected examples |
| Actor intent | Contemporaneous first-party record | Later inferred commentary |
| User experience | Appropriately sampled user evidence with method disclosure | One anecdote |

## Appraise

For every load-bearing source record:

- provenance, date, version, funding, and conflicts;
- design and comparison groups;
- population, setting, and directness to the target;
- outcome definition, measurement, baselines, and available raw data/code;
- sample size, variance, intervals, missing data, and sensitivity;
- independent replication and contradictory results; and
- whether the source reports observation or inference.

Downgrade for confounding, selective reporting, indirect settings, stale versions, imprecision,
undisclosed methods, conflicts, or unresolved contradictory evidence. Upgrade only for stronger
design or independent replication. Record the reason.

## Exercise Products

When current behavior matters and safe access exists, record version, account level, region, flags,
date, exact action, expected observable, and untouched output. Separate observation from inference.
Do not run when access terms, cost, safety, or private data make the exercise inappropriate.

## Rejections

Record a rejected source when another researcher could reasonably find and reuse it. Name the
specific failure: wrong identity, retraction, unavailable method, superseded version, population
mismatch, fabricated citation, or unresolved conflict. Do not reject by venue category alone.
