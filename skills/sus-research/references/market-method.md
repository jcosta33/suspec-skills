# Market Research

Use this reference for market, customer, competitor, UX, category, positioning, pricing, review,
market-sizing, and synthetic-respondent questions.

## Frame

Record the decision, audience or segment, geography, time horizon, and evidence that would change
the direction. Enterprise buyers, maintainers, hobbyists, and procurement teams are not one user.

## Source Map

Classify every source before using it:

- observed product behavior: direct use, current docs, changelog, pricing flow, screenshots;
- user evidence: interviews, surveys, reviews, support threads, community posts;
- primary research: experiments, panels, analytics, disclosed qualitative work;
- official market data: filings, government or industry datasets, standards bodies;
- secondary analysis: analyst reports, media, blogs, roundups; and
- synthetic output: hypothesis generation or calibrated augmentation only.

A source is strong only for claims inside its competence. A pricing page proves current price, not
customer value. Marketing copy proves positioning, not behavior. A review sample reports sampled
experience, not universal demand.

## Coverage Language

For "most," "common," "standard," or "users expect," disclose the searched population, denominator
when known, independent instances, and contrary cases. Sparse evidence supports "example observed"
or "candidate pattern," nothing broader. No fixed witness count converts a sample into a market fact.

## Product And Competitor Claims

Verify current behavior in the product, docs, changelog, pricing, API reference, app-store listing,
or dated screenshots. Record URL, access date, account/region/version limits, and the exact
observation. Treat landing-page claims as positioning only.

## Surveys And Interviews

Disclose sponsor, sample size, population, recruitment/source, dates, mode, screener, stimulus,
geography, segment, incentives, weighting/cleaning, response limits, and known bias. Do not attach
probability precision to a non-probability sample without the model and assumptions.

## Reviews

Disclose collection method, dates, platforms, sampling, duplicates, bots, incentives, moderation,
language filters, and missing populations. Keep quotations short and traceable. Never generalize one
thread or quote into a customer fact.

## Synthetic Evidence

LLM-generated respondents may brainstorm attributes, pilot stimuli, or augment a model calibrated
against real data. They are not customers and do not replace human evidence, especially for new
categories, segments, or willingness-to-pay. Label synthetic output and name the real-data check
needed next.

## Market Size

Show formula, units, source and date for every variable, segment boundary, assumptions, and at least
one sensitivity range. Separate TAM, SAM, and SOM. A precise number from weak inputs remains weak.

## Confidence

- **High:** direct, recent, segment-matched, independently triangulated.
- **Moderate:** direct but partial, or triangulated from mixed-quality sources.
- **Low:** indirect, stale, small-N, single-source, marketing-derived, or synthetic.
- **Very low:** assumption-heavy; use only to define the next test.

Name every downgrade: bias, indirectness, imprecision, age, conflict, weak sample, or synthetic-only
evidence. Surface disconfirming evidence.

## Output Check

Every major finding records observation or source claim, inference, evidence, confidence, decision
implication, and next test. The research may recommend. It never converts that recommendation into
a binding product decision.
